##### Groups.
**Def.** A set $G$ of elements with an operation $+: G \times G \to G$ is called a *group* if the following properties hold:
1. Associativity : $\forall a,b,c \in G \quad (a+b)+c=a+(b+c)$
2. Identity : $\exists e \in G \quad \forall g \in G$ s.t. $e+g=g+e=g$
3. Inverse : $\forall a \in G \quad \exists b \in G$ s.t. $a+b=b+a=e$

The group is called a commutative (Abelian) group if we have additionally 
$$
\forall a, b \in G \quad a+b=b+a
$$ 
Example: $S_n \coloneqq \{\pi : \{1, \dots, n\} \to \{1, \dots, n\} \mid \pi \text{ is bijective}\}$


##### Fields.
**Def.** A set $F$ with 2 operations $+, * : F \times F \to F$ is called a *field* if the following properties hold:
1. $(F, t)$ is a commutative group with identity element 0, $t = +, *$
2. $(F \setminus \{ 0 \}, *)$ is also a commutative group with identity element 1
3. Distributivity: $\forall a, b, c \in F \quad a*(b+c)=a*b + a*c$

Example: For a $n \in \mathbb{Z}$ consider $\mathbb{Z}_n \coloneqq \{ 0, 1, \dots, n-1 \}$ such that $a +_n b \coloneqq (a+b) \mod{n}$, $a *_n b \coloneqq (a*b)\mod n$. Then $(\mathbb{Z}, +_n, *_n)$ is a field iff $n\geq 1$ is prime, otherwise it will always be a *ring*.


##### Vector Spaces.
**Def.** Let $F$ be a field with identity elements 0 and 1. A vector space over the field $F$ is a set $V$ with a mapping $+ : V \times V \to V$ and $* : F \times V \to V$ then:
1. $(V, +)$ is a commutative group
2. Multiplicative identity : $\forall v \in V \quad 1*v = v$
3. Distributive property : $\forall a, b \in F$ and $\forall u, v \in V$ such that $a(u+v)=au + av$ and $(a+b)u=au+bu$

##### Subspace.
**Def.** Let $V$ be a vector space, $U \subset V$ non-empty set, we call $U$ a subspace of $V$ if it is closed under linear combination:
$$
\forall \lambda, \mu \in F \quad \forall u, v \in U \quad \lambda u + \mu v \in U
$$ 

##### Linear Combinations.
**Def.** $V$ vector space over $F$, $u_1, \dots, u_n \in V$, $\lambda_1, \dots, \lambda_n \in F$ then $\sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_i u_i$ is called a *Linear Combination*. The set of all linear combinations of $u_1, \dots, u_n \in V$ is called the *span* (linear hull) of $u_1, \dots, u_n \in V$.
$$
\text{Span}(u_1, \dots, u_n) \coloneqq \left\{ \sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_i u_i \mid \lambda_i \in F \right\}
$$ 
The set $U \coloneqq \{u_1, \dots, u_n\}$ is the *generator* of Span($u_1, \dots, u_n$). To find good generators, we need to know what is linear independence and dependence. 

##### Linear Independence.
**Def.** A set of vectors $v_1, \dots, v_n$ is called linearly independent if $\sum^n_{i=1} \lambda_i v_i =0 \implies \lambda_1 = \lambda_2 = \dots = \lambda_n = 0$

##### Basis.
**Def.** A subset $B$ of a vector space $V$ is called a (Hamel) basis if:
1. $\text{Span}(B) = V$
2. $B$ is linearly independent

**Proposition.** If $U=\{ u_1, \dots, u_n \}$ spans a vector space $V$, then the set $U$ can be reduced to the basis of $V$.

**Proof.** 
- If $U$ is already linearly independent : Done
- If $U$ is dependent : there exists $a \in U$ that is a linear combination of other vectors in $U$, we remove it.
- We keep removing all $a$ until the remaining set is linearly independent $\blacksquare$


**Def.** A vector space is called finite dimensional if it contains a finite basis.

**Prop.** Let $U = \{ u_1, \dots, u_n \} \subset V$ be a set of linearly independent vectors and $V$ be a finite dimensional vector space, then $U$ can be extended to a basis of $V$.

**Proof. (Sketch)**
Let $w_1, \dots, w_n$ be basis of $V$. Consider the set $\{ u_1, \dots, u_n, w_1, \dots, w_n \}$. Remove vectors from the set until remaining vectors are linearly independent.

**Corollary.** Let $V$ be a finite dimensional vector space then any two basis of $V$ have the same length.


##### Dimension.
**Def.** The length of the basis of a finite dimensional vector space is called the *dimension* of $V$.

**Def.** Assume that we have $U_1$, $U_2$ subspaces of $V$ , the sum of the two spaces is defined as :
$$
U_1 + U_2 \coloneqq \{ u_1 + u_2 \mid u_1\in U_1, u_2\in U_2 \}
$$ 
the sum is called the *direct sum*, if each element in the sum can be written in exactly one way.
$$
U_1 \oplus U_2 \quad \text{s.t.} \quad U_1 \cap U_2 = \{0\}
$$ 
This is a way of combining two (or more) subspaces into a larger vector space, in a way such that the decomposition of vectors is *unique*. For some vector space $V$ we can have have its unique $k$ constituents as follows:
$$
\begin{gather*}
\forall v \in V, \quad \exists u_1 \in U_1, u_2 \in U_2, \dots, u_k \in U_k \text{ such that } v = \sum_{i=1}^k u_i\\
V = \bigoplus_{i=1}^k U_i 
\end{gather*}
$$ 
**Prop.** Suppose $V$ is finite dimensional and $U \subset V$ is a subspace the there exists a subspace $W \subset V$ such that $U\oplus W = V$.

**Proof. (Sketch)** Let $\{ u_1, \dots, u_n \}$ basis of $U$ extend it to a basis of $V$, say the resulting set is 
$$
\{ \underbrace{u_1, \dots, u_n}_{U}, \underbrace{v_1, \dots, v_m}_{W} \}
$$ 
Define = $\text{Span}\{v_1, \dots, v_m\}$


##### Linear Mapping.

**Def.** Let $U$, $V$ vector space over $F$, a mapping $T:U\to V$ is called a linear if $\forall u_1, u_2 \in U$, $\forall \lambda \in F$
$$
\begin{gather*}
f(u_1 + u_2) = f(u_1) + f(u_2)\\
f(\lambda u_1) = \lambda f(u_1)
\end{gather*}
$$ 
the set of all linear mappings for $U\to V$ is denoted $\mathcal{L} (U, V)$. If $U\equiv V$ then  $\mathcal{L}(U)$.


**Def.** $T \in \mathcal{L}(U,V)$ then *kernel* (*nullspace*) of $T$ is defined as $\text{ker}(T) \coloneqq \text{null}(T) \coloneqq \{ u \in U \mid Tu=0 \}$ (all points that the transformation $T$ maps to 0).

**Prop.** 
- $\text{ker}(T)$ is a subspace of U.
- $T$ is injective iff $\text{ker}(T) = \{0\}$


**Def.** The range of $T$ is defined as $\text{range}(T) \coloneqq \text{Im}(T) \coloneqq \{ Tu \mid u \in U \}$.

**Prop.** The range is always a subspace of $V$ ($T$ is surjective iff $\text{range}(T)=V$)


**Def.** For any set $V^\prime \subset V$, the pre-image of $V^\prime$ is defined as
$$
T^{-1}(V^\prime) \coloneqq \{ u \in U \mid Tu \in V^\prime \}
$$ 

**Prop.** If $V^\prime \subset V$ is a subspace of $V$, then $T^{-1}(V^\prime)$ is a subspace of $U$

**Theorem.** Let $V$ be finite dimensional, $W$ any vector space, $T \in \mathcal{L}(V,W)$
Let $u_1, \dots, u_n$ be a basis of $\text{ker}(T) \in V$
Let $w_1, \dots, w_m$ be a basis of $\text{range}(T) \subset W$
Then $u_1, \dots, u_n, T^{-1}(w_1), \dots, T^{-1}(w_m) \subset V$ form a basis of $V$
In particular $\text{dim}(V)= \text{dim}(\text{ker}(T)) + \text{dim}(\text{range}(T))$

**Proof.** Denote $z_1 \coloneqq T^{-1}(w_1), \dots, z_m \coloneqq T^{-1}(w_m)$
![[subspace_proof.png]]
Step 1: $V \subset \text{Span}\{ u_1, \dots, u_n, z_1, \dots, z_m \}$ 
let $v\in V$, consider $Tv \in \text{range}(T)$
$\implies \exists \lambda_1, \dots, \lambda_m$ s.t. $Tv = \lambda_1 w_1 + \dots + \lambda_m w_m = \lambda_1 T(z_1) + \dots + \lambda_m T(z_m)$
$\implies Tv = T(\lambda_1 z_1+ \dots + \lambda_m z_m)$
$\implies Tv - T(\lambda_1 z_1+ \dots + \lambda_m z_m) = 0$
$\implies T(\underbrace{v - (\lambda_1 z_1+ \dots + \lambda_m z_m)}_{\in \text{ker}(T)})=0$
$\exists \mu_1, \dots \mu_n$ s.t. $v - (\lambda_1 z_1+ \dots + \lambda_m z_m) = \mu_1 u_1 + \dots$
$\implies v = \underbrace{\lambda_1 z_1+ \dots + \lambda_m z_m}_{\text{range}} + \underbrace{\mu_1 v_1 + \dots + \mu_n u_n}_{\text{ker}}$
Step 2: $u_1, \dots, u_n, z_1, \dots, z_m$ are linearly independent
Assume that
$\mu_1u_1 + \dots + \mu_n u_n + \lambda_1 z_1 + \dots + \lambda_m z_m = 0$   --(i)
$\mu_1u_1 + \dots + \mu_n u_n = \lambda_1 T(z_1) + \dots + \lambda_m T(z_m)$
$= \lambda_1 T(z_1) + \dots + \lambda_m T(z_m) + \underbrace{\mu_1 T(u_1) + \dots + \mu_n T(u_n)}_{=0}$
$T(\underbrace{\lambda_1 z_1 + \dots + \lambda_m z_m + \mu_1 u_1 + \dots + \mu_n u_n}_{=0 \text{ by (i)}})=0$
$\lambda_1 w_1 + \dots + \lambda_m w_m =0$ only if $\lambda_1=\dots = \lambda_m =0$ since $w_1, \dots, w_m$ are bases.

**Prop.** $T \in \mathcal{L}(V, W)$ where $V$, $W$ are finite dimensional. Then these following statements are equivalent
1. $T$ is injective
2. $T$ is surjective
3. $T$ is bijective 
$$
A=\begin{pmatrix}

\\
a_{m1} &\quad &a_{mn}
\end{pmatrix}
= (a_{ij})_{\begin{gather*}
\\

\end{gather*}}
$$ 
Consider $T \in \mathbb{L}(V, W)$, $V, W$ are finite dimensional

Let,
$v_1, \dots, v_n$ be a basis of $V$
$w_1, \dots, w_m$ be a basis of $W$

- $V = \lambda_1 v_1 + \dots + \lambda_n v_n$
$T(v)=T(\lambda_1 v_1 + \dots + \lambda_n v_n)=\lambda_1 T(v_1) + \dots +\lambda_n T(v_n)$

- Each image vector $T(v_j)$ can be expressed in basis $w_1, \dots, w_m$
There exists coefficients $a_{1j}, \dots, a_{mj}$ such that $T(v_j) = a_{ij} w_1 + \dots + a_{mj} w_m$

- We may now stack these coefficients in a matrix:

$m$ rows, one for each basis vector of $W$
$n$ columns, one for each basis vector of $V$
= matrix of mapping $T$ w.r.t. the basis $v_1, \dots, v_n$ of $V$ and $w_1, \dots, w_m$ of $W$

Notation: Let $T: V \to W$ be linear, let $B_V$ be a basis of $V$, $C_W$ basis of $W$.
We denote by $M(T, B_V, C_W)$ the matrix corresponding to $T$ w.r.t bases $B_V$ and $C_W$.

##### Properties of Matrices.

Let $V$, $W$ vector spaces consider the basis fixed. Let $S, T \in \mathcal{L}(V, W)$
- $M(S + T) = M(S)+M(T)$
- $M(\lambda S) = \lambda M(S)$
- For $v = \lambda_1 v_1 + \dots + \lambda_n v_n$ we have that
$$
\underbrace{T(v)}_{\text{image of V under T}} = \underbrace{M(T) \begin{pmatrix}\lambda_1\\ \dots\\ \lambda_n\end{pmatrix}}_{matrix vector product}
$$ 
where $v_1, \dots, v_n$ is basis of $V$.
- $T: U \to V$, $S: V \to W$ linear, then $M(S \circ T) = M(S)\cdot M(T)$
